## SERIES OVERVIEW

### Series Logline

### Mission Statement

Codicon Hacking exists to demystify the art, science, and culture of hacking for a generation that lives online but rarely understands the architecture beneath it. We believe technical literacy is a civic responsibility. We believe the stories of people who exploit, defend, and rebuild digital systems are among the most consequential narratives of our time — and that those stories deserve to be told with precision, respect, and an unblinking eye for complexity. This series does not choose sides between attacker and defender. It chooses truth.

### Core Premise

Codicon Hacking is a season-long, narrative-driven exploration of hacking culture, landmark cybersecurity breaches, the ethics of offensive and defensive security, and the human beings who populate this world. Each episode is an investigation — part documentary, part technical seminar, part cultural criticism. The series operates at the intersection of code and consequence: tracing the exact technical path of a breach while never losing sight of why it happened, who it affected, and what it revealed about systems — digital and human — that we all depend on.

Season 1 traces a deliberate arc: from the romanticized "lone wolf" hacker of the 1980s and 90s, through the professionalization of cybercrime, to the present era of nation-state actors, ransomware cartels, and the emerging battlefield of AI-assisted offense. By the season finale, the audience will understand not just what happened in history's defining breaches — but why the entire threat landscape is entering a new, more dangerous phase.

## TONE & VOICE

### Overall Tone

Codicon Hacking is **intelligent, irreverent, authoritative, and occasionally darkly humorous**. We take the subject matter seriously without being solemn. We respect our audience enough to be direct, accurate, and unsparing — but we never lose sight of the fact that this is a story, and stories require pace, tension, release, and the occasional well-timed joke. Think of the tone as a documentary directed by someone who was also a former penetration tester: rigorous on the facts, loose on the collar.

### Voice Principles

#### 1. Precision Over Hype

| **// PRINCIPLE 01**<br>We never overstate the severity of a vulnerability, the sophistication of an attacker, or the impact of a breach to generate excitement. Accuracy is the baseline; every technical claim is verifiable. When the real story is dramatic enough — and it usually is — we let it breathe. Amplification is a sign of insecurity in the material. We are not insecure in our material. |
| --- |

#### 2. Respect the Craft

| **// PRINCIPLE 02**<br>We treat hacking — even black-hat hacking — as a form of craft and intellectual pursuit. We do not sneer at attackers or write them off as criminals. We explain *how* they did what they did with the same attention and admiration a sports broadcaster gives to an exceptional athlete's technique. Moral judgment, if it comes at all, belongs to the listener. We provide the facts and the frame; the audience renders a verdict. |
| --- |

#### 3. Narrative Before Jargon

| **// PRINCIPLE 03**<br>Every technical explanation is preceded by a human or narrative hook. We never open a technical explanation with a term. We open with a scene, a person, a consequence — and then we arrive at the term that describes it. Jargon earns its place in the sentence; it does not begin the sentence. This principle applies even in Tier 3 (deep) technical segments — the protocol details follow the story of why those details mattered. |
| --- |

#### 4. Earned Complexity

| **// PRINCIPLE 04**<br>Technical depth is a reward, not a gate. We earn the right to go deep by establishing context, building trust, and setting expectations. Within a single episode, complexity escalates from accessible to expert-level — but the escalation is deliberate, announced (in tone if not in words), and always tethered to a narrative reason. No complexity is introduced without a "therefore" — a reason it matters right now, in this story. |
| --- |

#### 5. No Moralizing

| **// PRINCIPLE 05**<br>We present facts and let the audience decide. We do not editorialize about whether a hacktivist was "right." We do not produce episodes that arrive at predetermined moral conclusions. The Editorial segment at episode's end is the host's personal POV — clearly framed as such, never presented as received wisdom. The audience is smart enough to form their own views; it is patronizing to hand them one. |
| --- |

#### 6. Irreverence Without Nihilism

| **// PRINCIPLE 06**<br>We are allowed to find the absurdity in corporate security theater, the dark comedy of predictable human error, and the irony of governments building the same tools they then warn citizens about. But irreverence has a floor: we never make light of victims, data subjects, or people whose lives were materially damaged by a breach. The joke is on the system; never on the people the system failed. |
| --- |

### Forbidden Language and Framing

| **// PROHIBITED — DO NOT USE**<br>•  **"Terrifying" / "Shocking" / "Devastating"** — Sensationalist adjectives that substitute emotion for analysis. Replace with specific factual descriptions of impact.<br>•  **"Hackers could bring down the entire internet"** — Unfounded catastrophism. Scope claims must be precise and sourced.<br>•  **Unnamed "dark web"** references without context — The dark web is an infrastructure, not a horror location. Explain what it is and why it matters.<br>•  **"The government/Big Tech is hiding this"** — Conspiracy framing without evidence. If there is evidence of concealment, report it precisely.<br>•  **Glorifying criminal damage** — Explanatory framing ("here's how it was done, here's why it worked") is never the same as celebratory framing ("sick hack, bro"). The former is journalism; the latter is PR for crime.<br>•  **Attributing attacks without sourcing** — "Russia did this" requires sourcing to vetted threat intelligence. "A threat actor attributed to Russian state interests by [source] did this" is acceptable.<br>•  **Victim-blaming framing** — Organizations and individuals who suffered breaches are not inherently stupid or negligent. Systemic failures are more often the cause. |
| --- |

### Tone Calibration Per Segment Type

| **Segment** | **Tone Target** | **Energy Level** | **Key Calibration Note** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Cold Open** | Cinematic, urgent, slightly ominous | High | Hook before name-dropping. The audience should feel something before they understand anything. |
| **The Setup** | Warm, contextual, curious | Medium | This is the "let me tell you a story" moment. Slow down. Establish the world. |
| **Deep Dive** | Precise, methodical, fascinated | Medium-High | The host should sound like someone genuinely delighted by how something works, not like they're reading a report. |
| **Human Layer** | Empathetic, curious, non-judgmental | Medium-Low | This is the emotional core. Drop the pace. Let interview audio breathe. |
| **Countermeasure** | Constructive, expert, grounded | Medium | This is not a sales pitch for security products. It's a craft discussion. |
| **Editorial** | Personal, considered, provocative | Medium-High | The host takes a clear POV, but the framing must signal "this is my view" not "this is the truth." |
| **Outro / CTA** | Conversational, warm, anticipatory | Medium-Low | No hard sell. Tease, invite, and get out. Leave them wanting the next episode. |

## EPISODE STRUCTURE

### Standard Episode Blueprint

| // COLD OPEN — 0:00 – 2:00 |
| --- |

**Purpose:** Create an immediate, visceral hook before any context is provided. The audience should be hooked before they know what the episode is about.

- Open *in medias res*: a scene from the breach event, a transcript fragment, a single cryptic line of code, a real-world consequence moment.
- No music with lyrics. Sound design and silence only in this segment.
- Host does not appear on camera (or speaks only in voiceover) until the very end of the cold open.
- Ends with a question, a paradox, or a line that demands the episode answer it.
- Maximum 120 seconds. Under 90 preferred.
**Example structure:** [30 sec — re-enactment audio/visual] → [45 sec — voiceover narration, no names yet] → [15 sec — host on camera, one sentence hook] → [TITLE CARD + THEME]

| // THE SETUP — 2:00 – 8:00 |
| --- |

**Purpose:** Establish the who, where, when, and what before any technical content. This is the world-building segment.

- Host on camera, sitting or standing — not pacing. Grounded presence.
- Introduce the primary subject entity (company, agency, individual) and their context.
- Establish the time period and technological environment of the breach/event.
- Introduce any guest expert(s) with brief narrative integration (not a title card introduction).
- First use of all major jargon terms — defined immediately (Tier 1 depth).
- Ends with the "inciting incident" — the moment everything went wrong.

| // THE DEEP DIVE — 8:00 – 22:00 |
| --- |

**Purpose:** The technical core of the episode. Chronological reconstruction of the attack vector, exploit chain, propagation, and detection.

- Escalates from Tier 1 to Tier 2 (and occasionally Tier 3 for secondary audience segments).
- B-roll of screen recordings, animated diagrams, code snippets required throughout.
- Guest expert audio woven in as *narrators*, not talking heads — their words should feel like part of the story.
- Structured in clear sub-acts: *Initial Access → Lateral Movement → Exfiltration/Impact* (or equivalent).
- All technical claims verified and sourced before production.
- Minimum 1 on-screen code or tool visualization per major phase.

| **// EDITOR'S NOTE**<br>This is the longest segment. Pacing is critical. Use chapter-style chapter transitions (text-on-screen beat, brief musical sting) every 3–4 minutes to signal phase shifts and prevent attention drop-off. |
| --- |

| // THE HUMAN LAYER — 22:00 – 30:00 |
| --- |

**Purpose:** Shift from technical reconstruction to human consequence — the motivations of threat actors, the experience of victims, the culture and subculture context.

- Primary use of interview audio/video from actual subjects (where available) or carefully sourced court documents, chat logs, and public statements.
- Threat actors are presented with complexity — their motivations, backgrounds, and the subcultures that produced them.
- Victim experience is represented with empathy and specificity — not as abstract data points.
- This segment is the emotional center of the episode. Editing rhythm slows noticeably.
- Host narration in this segment is warm and restrained — less analytical, more present.

| // THE COUNTERMEASURE — 30:00 – 38:00 |
| --- |

**Purpose:** The defensive perspective — how the breach could have been prevented, how it was ultimately detected/contained, and what lessons the security community took from it.

- Guest expert (defender side) takes a larger role in this segment.
- Covers: patch/vulnerability lifecycle, detection gaps, incident response, post-breach remediation.
- Introduces ethical hacking or red team context where relevant.
- Avoids product placement framing — no sponsored security tools mentioned by name without disclosure.
- Ends with a clear "lesson statement" — one or two sentences the entire episode has been building toward.

| // THE EDITORIAL — 38:00 – 42:00 |
| --- |

**Purpose:** The host's personal analysis and the broader implications of this episode's events for the digital world at large.

- Host on camera, direct address to audience. No cuts to other voices in this segment.
- Clearly framed as the host's view: "Here's what I think this means..." not "Here's what this means."
- Connects to the season arc — this episode's story as one chapter in a larger narrative.
- May pose open questions without answering them (by design).
- Plants at least one seed for the next episode or season arc development.

| // OUTRO / CTA — 42:00 – 45:00 |
| --- |

**Purpose:** Close the episode, build community, and create forward momentum.

- Episode recap in 2–3 sentences max (for podcast listeners who need re-anchoring).
- Resources mentioned (links provided in show notes / description — not read aloud).
- Community question or discussion prompt for Discord/comments.
- Next episode tease: 20–30 seconds — curious, not spoiler-heavy.
- Consistent sign-off phrase (TBD by host in production — must feel authentic, not scripted).

### Special Episode Formats

| **Format** | **Structure** | **Duration** | **When to Deploy** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Deep Dive Solo** | Host only. No guests. Extended Deep Dive segment (up to 28 min). Removes Human Layer and Countermeasure as standalone segments — integrates both into the narrative. Requires stronger scripting. | 40–45 min | For well-documented historical events where interview sourcing is impractical. Best for technical elegance stories. |
| **Panel Debate** | Host + 2–3 expert guests. Structured debate format around a contested ethical or technical question. Setup and Editorial are retained; Deep Dive is replaced with moderated discussion. | 45–50 min | Once per season. Topics: responsible disclosure, offensive security ethics, AI-assisted hacking. |
| **Live CTF Coverage** | Real-time or near-real-time coverage of a CTF (Capture the Flag) competition. Play-by-play commentary on challenges. Post-event walkthrough of key solutions. | 60–90 min | Once per season. Airs live or within 24 hrs of event. High community engagement format. |
| **Field Report** | Host reporting from a location — a conference (DEF CON, Black Hat), a data center, a courthouse. On-location B-roll heavy. Interview-forward. Reduced scripting. | 35–45 min | When on-location access is available and adds genuine narrative value. |

## NARRATIVE ARCS

### Season-Level Arc

Season 1 of Codicon Hacking is structured as a macro-narrative titled internally: **"The Evolution of the Attacker."** Beneath each individual episode's story runs a season-wide throughline that tracks how the identity, motivation, and capability of cyber threat actors has transformed over four decades — from curious teenagers in bedrooms to coordinated nation-state operations, organized crime syndicates, and AI-augmented adversaries. The season does not tell this arc explicitly; it lets the evidence of each episode accumulate until the finale makes the arc undeniable.

| **Episode Block** | **Arc Phase** | **Thematic Thread** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Episodes 1–3 | **Origins** | The hacker ethos — curiosity, counter-culture, early internet freedom ideology. The lone wolf as folk hero. |
| Episodes 4–6 | **Professionalization** | The shift from hobbyists to mercenaries. The birth of cybercrime markets. The first government-sponsored operations. |
| Episodes 7–9 | **Industrialization** | Ransomware-as-a-Service. Supply chain attacks. Critical infrastructure targeting. The attacker becomes an industry. |
| Episodes 10–12 | **Reckoning** | The ethical frontier — who gets to hack back? What does defense look like when offense is a nation-state? The question of AI. |
| Episode 13 (Finale) | **The Next Attacker** | Synthesis: what the entire season's arc means for the next decade of cybersecurity. No clean resolution — only sharper questions. |

### Episode-Level Arc Requirements

Every standard episode must contain all four narrative beats, even if they overlap with the technical structure:

- **Setup:** A world in a state of false security or ignorance. Establish what people believed was true.
- **Complication:** The moment that belief is shattered. The intrusion, the vulnerability, the revelation.
- **Revelation:** What the complication reveals — about the attacker, the defender, the system, the culture.
- **Implication:** What this means going forward. Not a resolution — an opening. A door left ajar.

| **// WRITER'S NOTE**<br>These four beats are the DNA of each episode, not its skeleton. They can be distributed non-linearly. The Cold Open may deliver the Complication; the Revelation may arrive during the Human Layer rather than the Deep Dive. What matters is that all four are present before the Editorial begins. |
| --- |

### Character Arcs for Recurring Hosts/Analysts

In a non-fiction series, character arc belongs to the host's *understanding*, not their *personality*. Over the course of Season 1, the Lead Host should demonstrate a visible evolution:

- **Episodes 1–4:** Confident and analytical. The host knows this world.
- **Episodes 5–8:** The host is increasingly confronted with moral ambiguity they did not anticipate. Small moments of genuine uncertainty begin to surface in the Editorial.
- **Episodes 9–12:** The host is wrestling with the implications of what the season has revealed. The editorial voice becomes more personal, more urgent.
- **Finale:** The host does not arrive at answers. They arrive at better questions. This is the arc's resolution.
### Guest Narrative Integration

Guests are not interviewees — they are witnesses and narrators. Their contributions should feel like first-person chapters of the episode's story, not talking-head commentary. The following integration model applies:

- Guests are briefed with a narrative brief (see Appendix D), not just a topic list. They understand where their contribution falls in the episode's arc.
- Guest audio is edited into the body of the episode, not kept in a separate "interview" segment. The host's narration hands off to the guest's voice as a continuation of the story.
- Guest statements that express opinion are clearly framed as such in the edit (e.g., pre-empted by host narration: "And [Guest], who spent six years inside that system, told us...").
- No guest is introduced with a title card that reads like a resume line. Introduction happens through context: "The person who first discovered this wasn't a government analyst — it was [Name], a 26-year-old working a bug bounty in their apartment."
### Cliffhangers and Serialized Hooks

Codicon Hacking is a serialized series, not an anthology. Even episodes that tell complete, standalone stories must plant at least one narrative seed for subsequent episodes:

| **Hook Type** | **Technique** | **Frequency** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **The Loose Thread** | Mention a related actor, vulnerability, or event in passing — without resolution. Return to it 2–3 episodes later as a major thread. | Every episode |
| **The Contradiction** | End an episode with a fact or quote that contradicts the conventional narrative — without explaining it. Let the next episode deliver the explanation. | Every 3rd episode |
| **The Countdown** | Beginning at Episode 8, subtly reference that "something larger" is coming — without naming it. The season arc should feel like it's accelerating toward an endpoint. | Episodes 8–12 |
| **The Season Cliffhanger** | The finale ends without resolving the season's central question. A final piece of information is revealed — or a new question is raised — that can only be answered in Season 2. | Finale only (mandatory) |

## CHARACTER & HOST VOICE GUIDELINES

### Lead Host Profile

| **Attribute** | **Specification** |
| --- | --- |
| **Archetype** | The Investigator — relentlessly curious, forensically precise, comfortable with ambiguity. Not a pundit. Not a performer. A person genuinely trying to understand something difficult. |
| **Speaking Style** | Direct. Rhythmic. Occasional dry humor. Uses short declarative sentences during technical explanations; longer, more complex sentences during narrative. Never speeds through complexity. |
| **Areas of Authority** | Narrative structure, historical context, policy and cultural implications, ethical frameworks, interview technique. |
| **Areas to Defer to Guests** | Active exploit mechanics, current threat intelligence, specific tool usage, operational security practices. The host does not pretend to know things they don't know. |
| **On-Camera Presence** | Still and deliberate. No pacing. Eye contact with camera (not at notes) during editorial moments. Notes are permitted during technical segments — this signals authenticity. |
| **Humor Frequency** | Once per episode, maximum twice. Never at a victim's expense. Usually directed at the absurdity of institutional failure or the irony of a situation. |

### Co-Host / Analyst Profile

| **Attribute** | **Specification** |
| --- | --- |
| **Role** | The Technical Anchor. Appears in Deep Dive and Countermeasure segments. Provides ground-level expertise that the Lead Host can play against. |
| **Voice Relationship to Lead Host** | Complementary, not competitive. If the Lead Host is the storyteller, the Analyst is the annotator. They are not in agreement by default — productive friction is scripted and welcomed. |
| **Technical Depth** | Comfortable at Tier 2–3. Responsible for translating Tier 3 back to Tier 2 when the Lead Host asks clarifying questions (which they do, on behalf of the audience). |
| **Presence per Episode** | 10–15 minutes of the standard episode. More in Deep Dive Solo formats (via pre-recorded technical breakdowns). Less in Panel formats. |

### Guest Expert Framework

Guests fall into one of four roles. Their briefing, integration, and editorial handling should reflect their role classification:

| **Guest Role** | **Who They Are** | **Primary Segment** | **Briefing Emphasis** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **The Witness** | Someone who was present — the incident responder, the insider, the journalist who broke the story | Human Layer | What they experienced, saw, felt. Not technical mechanics. |
| **The Analyst** | Threat intelligence researcher, security engineer, academic | Deep Dive | Specific technical mechanism of the event. One or two specific technical points, not a survey. |
| **The Defender** | Red teamer, pentester, CISO, incident responder | Countermeasure | What could have been done differently. Lessons learned, not post-hoc criticism. |
| **The Philosopher** | Policy expert, ethicist, civil liberties advocate, journalist | Editorial / Panel | Broader implications. Invited to take a strong position. |

### Antagonist Framing

| **// EDITORIAL POLICY**<br>Threat actors — whether lone hackers, criminal cartels, or nation-state units — are presented as **complex agents operating within comprehensible motivations**, not as cartoon villains or misunderstood heroes. We explain their world: the economic incentives, the ideological frameworks, the technical environments that shaped them. We do not glorify their actions or minimize harm caused. The framing is: *"Here is what this person/group did, here is why they believed it was worth doing, here is what it cost."* The moral weight of that cost is left to the audience. |
| --- |

### Voice Consistency Rules

- **Pacing:** Host speaking rate targets 140–160 WPM for narrative; 120–130 WPM for technical explanation. Never rush through a new concept. Silence after a technical term is a feature.
- **Vocabulary level:** Default to the upper edge of general educated vocabulary. Do not use unnecessarily academic language; do not dumb down. The audience is smart.
- **Analogy use:** One strong analogy per major technical concept. Analogies are bridges, not decorations — use one that creates genuine understanding, then move on. Do not over-analogize.
- **Humor frequency:** Max 1–2 moments of levity per episode. Never forced. Never during victim narratives.
- **Technical acronyms:** Spell out on first use, every episode (even recurring terms — not every listener started at Episode 1).
### Prohibited Characterizations

| **// PROHIBITED FRAMINGS**<br>•  **"Genius lone hacker" trope** — Most significant attacks involve teams, prior research, and existing toolkits. The mythology of the lone prodigy is both factually inaccurate and narratively lazy.<br>•  **"Government is always the villain"** — Government actors are presented with the same analytical rigor as all others. State-sponsored attacks are documented and contextualized; systemic critique must be sourced.<br>•  **"Corporations are inherently evil"** — Security failures result from incentive structures, organizational complexity, and resourcing — not malice. Critique should be structural, not conspiratorial.<br>•  **"Hackers are always young, male, antisocial"** — The actual demographics of the hacking community are far more diverse. Visual representation and narrative framing must reflect this.<br>•  **Redemption arcs imposed on subjects** — If a subject did not express remorse or change, do not construct a redemption arc for them. Their complexity is not a problem to be solved. |

## TECHNICAL DEPTH REQUIREMENTS

### Technical Accuracy Standards

| **// ZERO TOLERANCE STANDARD**<br>**All technical claims in Codicon Hacking must be verifiable by a qualified security professional against at least one primary source.** Speculative attribution, unverified exploit details, or technical claims derived solely from journalism (without primary source corroboration) are not permitted without explicit disclosure. If we cannot verify a technical claim, we say so on air — and that acknowledgment becomes part of the story. |
| --- |

### Depth Tiers

| **Tier** | **Name** | **Audience Target** | **Technical Expectations** | **Example** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| TIER 1 | **Surface** | General tech audience, primary audience | All concepts explained using analogies. No unexplained acronyms. No assumed prior knowledge. | "A SQL injection attack is like slipping a fake question into a form that the database answers honestly — because it doesn't know the question was forged." |
| TIER 2 | **Mid** | Developer / IT / sysadmin audience | Proper terminology used without full definition. Assumes basic understanding of networking, authentication, and operating systems. | "The attackers exploited an unsanitized input field to inject arbitrary SQL — bypassing the authentication check entirely and returning the users table." |
| TIER 3 | **Deep** | Security professionals, researchers | Protocol-level detail, exploit mechanics, CVE references, tool names, MITRE ATT&CK framework mappings where applicable. | "The vulnerability — CVE-2021-44228, log4shell — allowed JNDI injection via crafted log messages. Exploitation triggered an outbound LDAP request to an attacker-controlled server, loading a malicious Java class." |

### Per-Segment Depth Mapping

| **Segment** | **Primary Depth Tier** | **Secondary Tier** | **Notes** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Cold Open | Tier 1 | — | No technical detail in the cold open. Atmosphere and narrative only. |
| The Setup | Tier 1 | — | All terms defined. Audience should feel informed, not tested. |
| The Deep Dive (early) | Tier 1–2 | — | Transition from Tier 1 to Tier 2 should be gradual, not abrupt. |
| The Deep Dive (mid/late) | Tier 2 | Tier 3 (clearly signaled) | Tier 3 moments should be brief, signaled ("for those of you who want to go deep here..."), and never gatekeeping. |
| The Human Layer | Tier 1 | — | Technical terminology drops significantly. The human story is the priority. |
| The Countermeasure | Tier 2 | Tier 1 | Defensive concepts must be actionable for the secondary audience, comprehensible for the primary. |
| The Editorial | Tier 1 | — | Editorial is accessible to everyone. No unexplained technical language. |

### Jargon Glossary Policy

- **First use:** Define immediately, in plain language, inline. Do not relegate definitions to a glossary card. The definition belongs in the sentence or the sentence immediately following.
- **Second use (same episode):** Use freely, without redefinition. The audience has been trusted to retain it.
- **Recurrence across episodes:** Core terms (e.g., zero-day, CVE, SQL injection) should be briefly re-defined in each episode where they feature prominently — not every episode, but whenever they are central. Not all listeners start at Episode 1.
- **Show notes glossary:** All Tier 2–3 terms used in an episode are listed in the show notes/video description with definitions and resource links.

### Fact-Checking Workflow and Source Hierarchy

1. **Primary Sources (Required for all technical claims):** NVD/CVE Database (nvd.nist.gov), NIST Special Publications, Vendor Security Advisories, CISA Alerts, Original researcher disclosure write-ups
2. **Secondary Sources (Acceptable for context and narrative):** Vetted security journalism (Krebs on Security, Wired, Ars Technica, The Register), Academic peer-reviewed papers, Congressional testimony and court documents
3. **Tertiary Sources (Background context only, never sole sourcing):** Blog posts from known security researchers, Podcast transcripts, Conference talks (DEF CON / Black Hat recordings)
4. **Prohibited Sources:** Anonymous social media claims, Unattributed forums, Marketing materials from security vendors, Press releases without corroboration

## PACING RULES

### Macro Pacing — Season Arc

| **Phase** | **Episodes** | **Pacing Character** | **Emotional Register** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Escalation** | 1–4 | Steady acceleration. Each episode's stakes slightly exceed the last. Technical complexity gradually increases. | Curiosity → Fascination |
| **Plateau** | 5–6 | A deliberate breath. One of these episodes should be the most accessible of the season — a reset before the turn. | Understanding → Reflection |
| **Pivot** | 7 | The season changes direction. A previously established assumption is overturned. Marks the transition from "history" to "present." | Discomfort → Re-orientation |
| **Climax** | 8–11 | Rapid escalation. Episodes are denser, stakes are higher. Pacing is tighter. The season arc becomes undeniable. | Urgency → Dread (productive) |
| **Resolution** | 12–13 | Deliberate, considered, unhurried. The finale earns its length. It does not rush to close loops — it names the loops we're left with. | Weight → Open questions |

### Episode-Level Pacing Rules

- **Cold Open cuts:** Maximum 3 cuts in 2 minutes. Slower than the audience expects — creates unease that the episode earns.
- **Setup transitions:** Match-cut or motivated cut only. No jump cuts in the Setup. The audience is being oriented; disorienting editing is counterproductive here.
- **Deep Dive rhythm:** Cut to B-roll (diagram, code, screen recording) every 45–90 seconds of talking head. No talking head shot longer than 90 seconds unbroken.
- **Human Layer editing:** Allow interview audio to run long. Resist the instinct to cut at every pause. Pauses carry emotional information — protect them.
- **Editorial pace:** Slower than any other segment. This is where the audience processes everything they've learned. One idea per sentence. Full stops. Breathe.
### Energy Curve Per Episode Format

| **Format** | **Energy Arc** | **Key Pacing Note** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Standard Episode** | High → Medium → High → Low → Medium → Low → Medium | The dip at Human Layer is intentional — it is the emotional center of gravity. |
| **Deep Dive Solo** | High → Sustained Medium-High → Declining → Low | Solo episodes must be ruthlessly scripted. Without guest voices to create variety, pacing becomes entirely editorial. 3-minute maximum on any single technical segment without a hard cut or tone shift. |
| **Panel Debate** | Medium → High (peak during debate) → Medium | Host must actively manage energy — prevent runaway agreement (boring) and prevent runaway conflict (uncomfortable). Productive tension is the target. |
| **Field Report** | Variable — environment-driven | Let the location set the energy. On-location audio is a feature. B-roll of environment justifies longer narration stretches. |

### Dead Air and Silence Policy

| **// SILENCE AS EDITORIAL TOOL**<br>Silence is not a production failure. In the Human Layer, a 3–5 second pause after a guest delivers a significant statement is an editorial choice that says: *what you just heard mattered*. In the Cold Open, 2–4 seconds of ambient audio before the first word creates pressure. In the Editorial, a beat of silence after a hard question is the host giving the audience space to sit with it. Editors are instructed to resist the urge to fill every pause. Silence earns its presence the same way a word does. |

## CONTENT STANDARDS & ETHICS

### Responsible Disclosure Policy

| **// HARD RULE — NO EXCEPTIONS**<br>**Codicon Hacking will not publish, describe, or reference specific technical exploit details for vulnerabilities that have not been publicly disclosed and patched.** If an episode covers an event involving an unpatched or recently patched vulnerability, the specific exploitation mechanism is described in general terms only. The editorial decision is: we can explain *what type* of attack was used and *what it achieved*, but not provide a reproducible methodology. If in doubt, consult legal and the technical fact-checker before scripting. |
| --- |

- All CVE references in content must link to the NVD entry in show notes.
- Proof-of-concept code references are limited to public, widely known, and patched exploits. No novel PoC is presented or implied.
- If a researcher guest shares information under embargo, that information does not appear in the episode until embargo lifts.
- When in doubt, the policy is conservative. The editorial value of a specific exploit detail rarely outweighs the potential harm of its disclosure.
### Source Protection Standards

- Anonymous sources and whistleblowers are handled under a documented source protection protocol maintained by the Executive Producer and legal counsel.
- Identity-masking in audio (voice alteration) must be disclosed to the audience in the episode and show notes ("This individual's voice has been altered to protect their identity.").
- No source is identified without their explicit written consent, reviewed by production legal.
- Digital communications with confidential sources use end-to-end encrypted channels only. Source contact details are stored only by the Executive Producer, not in shared production systems.
- If a source's identity is later publicly disclosed by a third party, we do not confirm or deny our use of that source.
### Legal Review Triggers

The following content categories require legal review and sign-off before the episode script is finalized:

| **Trigger** | **Reason** | **Review SLA** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Naming a specific living individual as a threat actor | Defamation risk; attribution must be legally defensible | 5 business days |
| Any re-enactment or dramatization | Misleading framing risk; must be clearly labeled | 3 business days |
| Use of leaked or unlawfully obtained documents | Potential receipt of stolen property; editorial privilege assessment required | 5 business days |
| Content involving minors (victims or threat actors) | Privacy and COPPA considerations | 5 business days |
| Episodes covering active law enforcement investigations | Potential witness intimidation or interference risk | 5 business days |
| Sponsored content or brand integrations | FTC disclosure compliance | 3 business days |

### Re-Enactment and Dramatization Standards

- All dramatized or re-enacted sequences must carry an on-screen label: DRAMATIZATION or RE-ENACTMENT — displayed for the full duration of the sequence, not just at the start.
- Re-enactments must not depict or imply events that are not supported by source documentation.
- Actors portraying real individuals must not be named or presented in a way that makes them visually identifiable as the real person.
- Composite characters (combining attributes of multiple real individuals) must be disclosed in the episode or show notes.
### Consent and Release Requirements

- All on-camera interview subjects sign a written release form before recording begins. Release is reviewed by production legal.
- Release forms specify: rights granted, any limitations on use (e.g., audio only, no facial reveal), right of subject to withdraw before publication.
- Anonymous sources who provide audio/voice content but wish not to appear on camera complete a separate limited-use release.
- Archival footage of private individuals requires clearance review. Public figures in their professional capacity do not require individual clearance for archival footage, but context must be accurate.
- All release documentation is retained for minimum 7 years post-publication.
